# PURPOSE

This directive establishes standardized Coalition defensive doctrine regarding the construction, maintenance, hardening, compartmentalization, and strategic utilization of infrastructure during hostile action, insurgency, invasion, sabotage events, or continuity-threatening emergencies.

The Coalition recognizes infrastructure not merely as civilian utility, but as the foundation of territorial survival.

Every structure is therefore to be considered strategically relevant under continuity conditions.

---

# SECTION I — STRATEGIC DOCTRINE

## §1.1 Infrastructure as Defense

Coalition doctrine recognizes all major infrastructure as dual-purpose assets capable of supporting both civilian continuity and defensive military operations.

Infrastructure shall therefore be designed to:

* Survive attack
* Delay hostile movement
* Support BWARS operations
* Preserve continuity
* Maintain communications
* Protect strategic personnel
* Enable fallback operations
* Restrict unauthorized movement

---

## §1.2 Distributed Defense Philosophy

The Coalition rejects overreliance upon singular defensive strongholds.

Continuity shall instead be maintained through:

* Layered defensive districts
* Redundant infrastructure
* Underground fallback systems
* Distributed supply storage
* Multiple command sectors
* Interconnected transit corridors
* Hardened strategic facilities

No single strike shall be capable of collapsing Coalition continuity.

---

# SECTION II — URBAN DEFENSIVE DESIGN

## §2.1 Strategic Urban Layout

Population centers shall be developed with defensive continuity considerations integrated into planning.

Urban sectors should incorporate:

* Narrow access corridors
* Elevated observation points
* Reinforced intersections
* Segmented district access
* Controlled chokepoints
* Surveillance overlap
* Emergency barricade capability

Open terrain directly surrounding critical infrastructure should be minimized where practical.

---

## §2.2 Checkpoint Integration

Major transportation corridors and district entrances shall support rapid BWARS checkpoint deployment.

Infrastructure should include:

* Retractable barriers
* Reinforced access gates
* Inspection platforms
* Vehicle denial systems
* Blast-resistant guard sectors
* Elevated observation access

Checkpoint infrastructure shall remain operational during power disruptions where possible.

---

## §2.3 Structural Hardening

Strategic facilities should incorporate reinforced construction techniques including:

* Obsidian shielding
* Layered blast barriers
* Underground reinforcement
* Redundant structural supports
* Emergency isolation doors
* Protected utility routing
* Fire containment sectors

Hardening priority shall be assigned to:

* Treasury facilities
* Communications hubs
* BWARS stations
* Rail systems
* Power infrastructure
* Intelligence sectors
* Underground corridors

---

# SECTION III — UNDERGROUND CONTINUITY SYSTEMS

## §3.1 Underground Infrastructure

Subterranean infrastructure shall serve as the primary continuity fallback environment during large-scale attack or surface instability.

Approved underground sectors may include:

* Transit corridors
* Supply vaults
* Personnel shelters
* Tactical staging areas
* Emergency command sectors
* Hidden communications relays
* Deep archive facilities

Underground systems shall remain compartmentalized where possible.

---

## §3.2 Emergency Isolation

Underground sectors shall possess capability for rapid isolation in the event of:

* Structural breach
* Hostile infiltration
* Radiation exposure
* Biological contamination
* Internal sabotage
* Civil unrest

Isolation systems may include:

* Blast doors
* Rail shutdown systems
* Flood barriers
* Airlock sectors
* Manual lockout controls
* Segment collapses

---

## §3.3 Tunnel Denial

Coalition doctrine recognizes underground tunnel systems as both strategic assets and potential vulnerabilities.

Where hostile use is suspected, authorized personnel may deploy:

* Controlled collapse operations
* Structural demolition
* Flood containment
* Redstone disablement
* Thermal denial systems
* Access sealing procedures

Preservation of continuity supersedes preservation of infrastructure.

---

# SECTION IV — INFRASTRUCTURE REDUNDANCY

## §4.1 Power Redundancy

Critical facilities shall maintain secondary or tertiary power systems where practical.

Protected systems should include:

* Treasury operations
* Communications arrays
* BWARS command sectors
* Surveillance systems
* Rail infrastructure
* Orbital uplink systems
* Strategic containment sectors

---

## §4.2 Communications Redundancy

Communication systems shall maintain fallback capability through:

* Underground relays
* Hardened transmission lines
* Isolated broadcast sectors
* Emergency signal repeaters
* Encrypted tactical networks

No strategic district shall depend upon a singular communication route.

---

## §4.3 Transportation Redundancy

Coalition transportation doctrine requires multiple fallback pathways between strategic sectors.

Transportation continuity should include:

* Rail alternatives
* Underground bypass routes
* Emergency access tunnels
* Reinforced bridges
* Hardened maintenance corridors

Transportation collapse is recognized as a continuity threat.

---

# SECTION V — CIVILIAN CONTINUITY

## §5.1 Emergency Shelter Doctrine

Residential sectors and major public facilities should incorporate emergency shelter capability where feasible.

Shelter sectors may include:

* Underground rooms
* Reinforced basements
* Emergency storage areas
* Water reserves
* Food storage
* Medical supply sectors

Shelter integration shall prioritize population stability during prolonged disruptions.

---

## §5.2 Controlled Evacuation Routes

Population movement during emergencies shall remain centralized and controllable.

Evacuation corridors should:

* Avoid strategic infrastructure
* Support BWARS oversight
* Minimize congestion
* Restrict unauthorized divergence
* Maintain surveillance visibility

Uncontrolled civilian migration is considered destabilizing.

---

# SECTION VI — STRATEGIC DENIAL AUTHORITY

## §6.1 Controlled Demolition

Where infrastructure capture by hostile forces becomes likely, authorized command personnel may approve:

* Structural demolition
* Tunnel collapse
* Rail destruction
* Communications denial
* Power interruption
* Access shaft sealing

Such actions shall prioritize denial of strategic advantage to hostile entities.

---

## §6.2 Infrastructure Preservation Hierarchy

During continuity emergencies, preservation priority shall be assigned in the following order:

1. Continuity command infrastructure
2. Strategic communications
3. Treasury systems
4. BWARS operational capability
5. Orbital and intelligence systems
6. Transportation infrastructure
7. Civilian industrial infrastructure
8. Nonessential structures

Civilian structures may be sacrificed where necessary to preserve continuity assets.

---

# SECTION VII — ENFORCEMENT AND COMPLIANCE

BWARS Infrastructure Security Divisions shall conduct periodic inspections regarding:

* Structural hardening compliance
* Underground continuity readiness
* Emergency access systems
* Redundancy capability
* Surveillance integration
* Defensive preparedness

Facilities failing compliance standards may face:

* Forced reconstruction
* Administrative seizure
* Operational restriction
* Security reassignment
* Tribunal review

---

# SECTION VIII — STRATEGIC JUSTIFICATION

The Coalition recognizes that modern warfare targets infrastructure before territory.

A city without power collapses.
A rail system without redundancy fractures.
A treasury without protection destabilizes governance.
A government without fallback systems ceases to function.

Infrastructure is therefore recognized as the first and last defensive line of continuity.
